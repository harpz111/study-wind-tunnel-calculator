Study Wind Tunnel Calculator

Preliminary sizing tool for open-circuit, low-speed wind tunnels (contraction → test section → diffuser).

Overview

This tool implements a simplified but physically consistent method for the preliminary sizing of open-circuit, low-speed wind tunnels. All sections are defined relative to the test section, which provides the reference flow conditions and the scaling for loss and power estimates. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

For a specified test-section Mach number and geometry, the method propagates flow conditions through the contraction, test section, and diffuser, then combines section losses into a single total loss coefficient. From this, it forms an energy ratio and estimates overall pressure losses and required motor power. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Method

The tunnel is modelled as three sections:

  • Contraction
  • Test section
  • Diffuser

The implementation follows a consistent workflow: determine local Mach number, then local Reynolds number, then friction factor, then section loss coefficients, and finally combine losses into a total energy estimate. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

The relations below summarise the core geometry, blockage, and loss modelling used by the calculator. (Full derivations and definitions are captured in the accompanying notes document.) :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Scope and conventions

Open-circuit study tunnel with three sections only: Contraction → Test section → Diffuser. Coordinates use X (width), Y (height), Z (streamwise length). :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Geometry (test-section referenced)

R_t = Y_t / X_t and A_t = X_t Y_t = R_t X_t², with Z_t = 2 X_t. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

R_c = A_{c,i} / A_{c,o}, with A_{c,o}=A_t and A_{c,i}=R_c A_t. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

R_d = A_{d,o} / A_{d,i}, with A_{d,i}=A_t and A_{d,o}=R_d A_t. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Diffuser length uses the expansion angle θ_e: Z_d = (Y_{d,o} − Y_t) / (2 tan θ_e). :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Total length relation (used for “fit-to-length” sizing): Z_w = (X_t/2) [ 3√(R_t/R_c) + 4 + (√(R_d R_t) − 1)/tan(θ_e) ]. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Volumetric flow

Test-section speed is set from Mach number: V_t = M_t a with a = √(γ R T), giving V̇_t = A_t V_t. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Boundary-layer blockage check

Blockage is computed from the boundary-layer displacement estimate and checked against the criterion BL ≤ 0.07. Both laminar (Blasius) and turbulent (1/7th power law) expressions are defined in the notes; the calculator uses the relevant regime logic and reports the result. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

Loss modelling and “energy ratio”

Each section loss is expressed in the standard non-dimensional form ΔP_i = k_i (½ ρ_i V_i²), then referenced back to test-section dynamic pressure to allow a consistent sum of losses. An energy ratio is defined from the test-section power scale and the total tunnel loss rate, giving a simple relationship between test-section operating point and motor power. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

Test-section power scale: P_t = ½ ṁ_t V_t². :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}

Total loss rate: P_c = Σ (k_{L,t} P_t), then energy ratio: E_r = P_t / P_c = 1 / Σk (in the simplified summed form used in the implementation). :contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}

Local Mach → Reynolds → friction factor workflow

For each section, the notes define an iterative local Mach relation (area-averaged), then a Reynolds relation, then the Prandtl universal law of friction (iterative) to obtain the Darcy friction factor. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

Hydraulic diameter for a rectangle: D_h = 2XY/(X+Y), and D_t = 2X_tY_t/(X_t+Y_t). :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}

Test-section loss: k_t = f_t (Z_t/D_t). :contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}

Diffuser split loss: k_d = k_f + k_ex with k_f = (1 − 1/R_d²) f_d / (8 sin θ_e) and k_ex = k_e(θ_e) ((R_d − 1)/R_d)², where k_e(θ_e) is piecewise in the notes. :contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}

Contraction loss (simplified, square inlet assumption in the notes): k_c = 0.32 f_c. :contentReference[oaicite:20]{index=20}

Design guidance and validation references

In addition to the formula set above, you are explicitly referencing empirical wind-tunnel loss data and design guidance from the literature. In particular:

  • NASA NTRS 19770005050 is used as a supporting source for empirical loss coefficients, including contraction losses and their dependence on area ratios, and for cross-checking the overall loss/energy approach.
  • DURMUŞ (2024), Optimization of contraction cone length in an open-circuit wind tunnel, is cited for explicit investigation of contraction length expressed in hydraulic-radius form (L ≈ 1R_i, 2R_i, 3R_i) and for the stated compromise trend that L ≈ 2R_i provides a strong balance between flow quality and wall-shear growth.
  • Pope & Barlow, Low-Speed Wind Tunnel Testing, is referenced for diffuser design heuristics (including discussion of diffuser expansion angle and commonly used diffuser area ratios such as R_d ≈ 3 for practical low-speed layouts).

The notes also include a comparison statement that the computed energy ratios can agree well with measured values for many facilities, with larger discrepancies expected for non-standard layouts or extra components not included in the simplified model. :contentReference[oaicite:21]{index=21}

Intended use

This tool is intended for rough preliminary sizing: early-stage feasibility, comparing candidate geometries, and estimating order-of-magnitude requirements (length, footprint, loss level, and motor power) from standard relations. :contentReference[oaicite:22]{index=22}

Limitations

The simplified model does not include losses associated with screens/honeycombs, corners/turning vanes, inlet/exit effects, fan performance curves, acoustic considerations, or structural constraints. It does not replace detailed CFD, hardware design, or experimental validation. :contentReference[oaicite:23]{index=23}

Boundary-layer blockage checks should be interpreted within the validity limits of the underlying correlations, and any final design should be validated with higher-fidelity analysis and/or measurements. :contentReference[oaicite:24]{index=24}
